### [Norm/Topic Sentence] Topic sentence
Begin each paragraph with a topic sentence stating the main idea; following sentences support it.
Why: helps the reader grasp the paragraph's point and improves coherence.
Example: "Technology has changed how teenagers socialize. First, ..."

### [Norm/Thesis] Clear thesis statement
State your main position clearly in the introduction so the reader knows your stance.
Why: an essay without a thesis reads as a list of unconnected ideas.
Example: "This essay argues that limiting screen time benefits teenagers."

### [Norm/Transitions] Cohesive devices / transitions
Use linking words (however, therefore, moreover, in contrast, for example) to connect ideas.
Why: signals logical relationships and improves flow (cohesion).
Example: "Technology saves time. However, it can also isolate people."

### [Norm/Paragraph Structure] Paragraph development (PEE)
Develop each paragraph as Point–Evidence–Explanation: claim, support, then interpretation.
Why: ensures arguments are substantiated rather than asserted.

### [Norm/Conclusion] Conclusion
Summarize the key points and restate the thesis; do not introduce new arguments.
Why: gives the essay closure and reinforces the main message.

### [Norm/Register] Academic register
Avoid contractions (don't -> do not), slang, and overly conversational phrasing in academic essays.
Why: maintains a formal, objective tone appropriate to the genre.
Example: "a lot of" -> "many / a considerable number of".

### [Norm/Precision] Precise word choice
Replace vague words (thing, stuff, good, bad) with specific terms.
Why: increases clarity and demonstrates lexical range.
Example: "a good effect" -> "a beneficial effect".

### [Norm/Repetition] Avoid repetition
Vary vocabulary and sentence openings instead of repeating the same words (people, important).
Why: repetition lowers perceived vocabulary range and reads monotonously.

### [Norm/Sentence Variety] Sentence variety
Mix simple, compound, and complex sentences; avoid stringing only short or only long sentences.
Why: improves readability and demonstrates syntactic control.

### [Norm/Coherence] Logical ordering
Order ideas logically (chronological, cause-effect, or by importance) and keep one idea per paragraph.
Why: a clear structure makes the argument easy to follow.
